When to Use It

After teams have developed their design plans and before they begin building. Can also be used mid-build when a team is stuck or wants to stress-test a decision.

Remember

Feedback is a gift. Stay specific — vague comments like "looks good" or "I don't get it" don't help anyone improve. The goal is to make the design stronger, not to be nice or mean.

Roles

Presenter: Share your plan clearly. Listen without interrupting.
Reviewer: Follow the TAG structure. Be honest and specific.

Teacher Background · Engineer
T.A.G. Feedback — Facilitator Guide
Analysis, inquiry, and structured peer critique · NGSS SEP 8 · CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.1

Standards Alignment

NGSS SEP 8 Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information — students analyze designs against criteria and communicate evaluation in structured form.
CCSS SL.1 Collaborative discussion — students engage with diverse partners, build on others' ideas, and express their own clearly.
NGSS ETS1-2 Evaluating solutions using criteria and constraints — the feedback structure prompts reviewers to evaluate feasibility and alignment explicitly.
C3 D4.6 Constructing explanations and arguments — students justify their feedback with evidence from the design.

Why This Routine — SOLD Alignment

S
Social: Learning happens through structured peer dialogue. TAG gives students a shared language for critique, making feedback feel safe and purposeful rather than personal.
O
Ownership: Designers retain agency — they receive input but decide what to accept, revise, or reject. This mirrors authentic professional practice.
L
Lived Experience: Students bring their knowledge of the community and challenge context to their questions and suggestions, grounding critique in real stakes.
D
Disciplinary Thinking: Engineers and designers routinely peer-review plans before production. TAG authentically replicates this professional norm.

Marzano Connection

  • Providing Feedback: TAG externalizes the feedback process — students learn to give the kind of specific, criteria-referenced feedback that research shows accelerates learning.
  • Cooperative Learning: The structured partner format prevents dominance; all voices participate in a predictable, equitable sequence.
  • Generating & Testing Hypotheses: Ask questions probe design assumptions, asking reviewers to predict failure modes and test feasibility claims.

Facilitation Notes

  • Model TAG once with a volunteer presenter before student pairs start — especially the Ask step, which students often default to criticism rather than genuine questions.
  • Require the designer to stay silent during T and A. Give them a notes sheet to capture feedback. This prevents defensive back-and-forth.
  • With younger scholars, scaffold the Tell step first by having the whole class identify one strength before pairs separate.
  • Invite professional partners to serve as reviewers. Brief them on the TAG protocol beforehand so their feedback follows the same structure.
  • After TAG, give 5–8 minutes for teams to revise their plan before building. Don't skip this — the revision moment is where learning consolidates.

Process Check
Briefly check in on how your team is working together before diving in.
What's working well on our team?
What might we need to adjust?
What do we need from each other today?

When to Use It

Every single build session, at the very start. Even on days when you feel like you already know the plan — checking in keeps the team aligned and accountable to the criteria.

The Point

Rushing into building without a plan wastes time and materials. The Production Plan helps you work like a real professional: deliberate, criteria-focused, and aware of your teammates.

Where to Record It

Use the Daily Log in your Engineer Team Notebook. Record your goal for the day before you start building. At the end of the session, note what you completed and what's next.

Teacher Background · Engineer
Production Plan — Facilitator Guide
Strategic planning, collaboration, metacognition · NGSS SEP 6 · CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.5 · C3 D4

Standards Alignment

NGSS SEP 6 Constructing explanations and designing solutions — daily planning against criteria mirrors the iterative design process in authentic engineering.
CCSS W.5 Production and distribution of writing — students develop and strengthen planning through reflection and revision of their daily logs over time.
C3 D4 Taking informed action — students translate criteria knowledge into actionable, coordinated work steps each session.
Math MP.6 Attend to precision — the criteria check develops habits of precision in materials, measurements, and fit-to-standard.

Why This Routine — SOLD Alignment

S
Social: The Process Check is explicitly relational — it names team health as a daily priority, not just task completion. This reduces friction before it escalates.
O
Ownership: Students set their own daily goals rather than following a teacher-issued task list. This builds executive function and professional self-management.
L
Lived Experience: Criteria connect to the real community challenge. Checking them daily grounds the build in purpose — it's not an abstract project, it's a response to real people.
D
Disciplinary Thinking: Real engineers begin every work session by reviewing specs, setting tasks, and checking team status. This routine teaches professional practice authentically.

Marzano Connection

  • Setting Objectives: Daily goal-setting against criteria is one of Marzano's highest-yield strategies. Students who set specific session goals outperform those given open build time.
  • Reinforcing Effort: The Process Check surfaces collaboration patterns and gives students language to name and address effort challenges within the team.
  • Cooperative Learning: Role clarity (who does what) within the structured check-in prevents the "one person does everything" problem common in group work.

Facilitation Notes

  • Protect this time — 5–10 minutes of upfront planning consistently outperforms unstructured build starts. Announce and enforce it as a non-negotiable.
  • Circulate during the Production Plan and use what you hear to target your coaching during the build session. Teams who set vague goals need tighter accountability.
  • Reference the criteria explicitly when teams check in. Prompt: "Show me in your criteria which one you're working toward today."
  • For younger scholars, provide a simple Production Plan card at each table with the three prompts printed. They can write directly on it each day.
  • At the end of sessions, invite one or two teams to share what they completed. This public accountability reinforces the planning habit.
