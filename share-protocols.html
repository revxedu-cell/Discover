What to Bring

Gather work from all five phases — notes, exit tickets, data, drafts. Your notebooks, task sheets, and performance tasks from each phase are your source material for this routine.

What You're Building Toward

The Explanation of Learning is a public statement of what you know, how you know it, and why it matters. Project Rewind gives you the raw material — the evidence — to make that explanation credible and specific.

The Point

Real expertise means being able to explain your thinking, not just show your final product. Project Rewind helps you connect the work you did to the learning you can now claim as yours.

Teacher Background · Share
Project Rewind — Facilitator Guide
Metacognition and synthesis · NGSS SEP 6, SEP 7 · CCSS ELA SL.1, SL.4

Standards Alignment

NGSS SEP 6 Constructing explanations — students synthesize cross-phase evidence into a coherent explanation of their learning process and impact.
NGSS SEP 7 Engaging in argument from evidence — students select specific work samples to support claims about their growth and their solution's effectiveness.
CCSS SL.1 Collaborative discussion — the pair-reflection option supports students in processing growth through structured dialogue.
CCSS SL.4 Presenting information — Project Rewind directly prepares students to organize and adapt their learning for public presentation to a diverse audience.

Why This Routine — SOLD Alignment

S
Social: The optional pair-reflection step acknowledges that growth is often recognized through conversation. A partner can name shifts they witnessed that the individual doesn't see in themselves.
O
Ownership: Students select their own evidence and craft their own explanation. They are the authors of their learning story — not passive participants reading a script.
L
Lived Experience: The "This work matters to…" prompt grounds the explanation in the real community and challenge. Share is not abstract — it's rooted in actual impact.
D
Disciplinary Thinking: Professionals present portfolios, defend dissertations, and explain their design process. Project Rewind teaches the metacognitive and rhetorical skills those high-stakes contexts demand.

Marzano Connection

  • Summarizing and Note-Taking: Trace Your Learning requires students to distill each phase into two essential ideas — a high-yield summarizing practice that improves retention and prepares synthesis.
  • Setting Objectives and Providing Feedback: The Explanation draft created in step 3 becomes a planning document for rehearsal and revision. Share is more powerful when students know what they're trying to explain before they practice.
  • Reinforcing Effort and Providing Recognition: The Reflect step invites students to name their growth explicitly — building self-efficacy that is rarely made visible in traditional assessments.

Facilitation Notes

  • Have students collect and physically gather their phase artifacts before Session 1 begins — notebooks, exit tickets, performance tasks. Without sources, the Trace step becomes guesswork.
  • Normalize that the draft in step 3 is rough. Say: "This is not your speech. It's your source material. We'll shape it in Session 2." This reduces perfectionism paralysis.
  • For the Reflect step, pair students strategically — ideally with someone they've worked closely with across the project. Peer recognition of growth is powerful and often more credible than teacher recognition to students.
  • Share out 2–3 Explanation openings from step 3 — model what "specific + evidence-based" sounds like versus "we learned a lot." This sets the standard for the full preparation session that follows.
  • Students who struggle with "Before I thought…/Now I think…" may need to review their early Discover work specifically. Their first Think-Feel-Act or A.S.K. Questions responses are often the clearest evidence of early thinking.

Same Routine, New Context

You've used T.A.G. Feedback in Engineer to improve your design plans. Here, the same structure helps you improve your explanation — the way you communicate your thinking, evidence, and growth. The logic is the same: be specific, be honest, be helpful.

The Presenter's Job

Stay in listening mode during T and A. Write down feedback rather than responding to it. You decide later what to keep, revise, or let go — this is your story.

The Reviewer's Job

Listen as if you're an audience member who doesn't already know what happened. When something is unclear, that's a gift — say so. Your job is to help the presenter be understood, not just praised.

After TAG

Take 5–8 minutes to revise your Explanation draft based on the feedback. Even one specific revision before Share will make a difference in how confident and clear you feel.

Teacher Background · Share
T.A.G. Feedback (Share) — Facilitator Guide
Analysis, inquiry, communicative precision · NGSS SEP 8 · CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.1, SL.4

Standards Alignment

NGSS SEP 8 Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information — reviewers evaluate the clarity, accuracy, and evidence-quality of a peer's public explanation.
CCSS SL.1 Collaborative discussion — students practice the listening and dialogic skills required to engage with an outside audience during Share.
CCSS SL.4 Presenting information — feedback directly targets clarity and organization of the Explanation, building toward a polished public performance.
CCSS W.5 Developing writing through revision — the feedback cycle produces revised Explanation drafts, treating the presentation as a writing/speaking product that benefits from iteration.

Why This Routine — SOLD Alignment

S
Social: Rehearsing with a partner replicates the social dynamics of the actual Share event. Feedback from a peer is often more useful than teacher feedback at this stage — and teaches students to receive critique professionally.
O
Ownership: The presenter decides what to revise. This preserves authorship — Share is their story, and TAG gives them expert input without prescribing the outcome.
L
Lived Experience: Ask questions simulate the perspective of a real audience member who doesn't share the student's context. This helps students bridge the gap between insider knowledge and public communication.
D
Disciplinary Thinking: Before public presentations, researchers, designers, and professionals conduct peer reviews of their talks. TAG at the rehearsal stage is authentic disciplinary practice.

Marzano Connection

  • Providing Feedback: TAGs in this context target communication quality — specificity, evidence use, and audience awareness — all of which are formative feedback categories that transfer to standardized writing and speaking tasks.
  • Reinforcing Effort: The Tell step ensures that rehearsals start with genuine recognition. Students who feel heard are more likely to revise authentically rather than defensively.
  • Cooperative Learning: The structured pair format prevents performance anxiety — when everyone is a reviewer and a presenter, the social stakes are equalized.

Facilitation Notes

  • Brief reviewers on what makes a Share Explanation strong before TAG begins — clarity, specificity, evidence use, and growth reflection. Give them the Share Feedback Checklist as a guide.
  • Model a full TAG round with a volunteer presenter before pairs break out. Show what a strong Ask question sounds like — students often default to simple clarification questions rather than probing ones.
  • Protect the revision time after TAG. Even 5 minutes of focused revision is worth more than additional rehearsal without reflection.
  • Consider inviting a community partner or parent to participate as a TAG reviewer. Their perspective as an outsider makes Ask questions especially valuable — they literally don't know the context, which mirrors the real audience.
  • For students who freeze in the presenter role, allow them to share in the T step first (as reviewer), then switch. Naming strengths in someone else's explanation often helps students see what's possible in their own.
