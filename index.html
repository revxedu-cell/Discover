Designing the Unit

Before you begin the five phases, there are a few foundational design decisions to work through. The following guidance supports that process.

Start with something real

A strong DEEDS unit is anchored in a challenge that exists in the world, not just in school. It should be real enough to matter, local enough for scholars to care, and substantial enough to require the learning teachers are responsible for.

Ground the work in an actual need, tension, problem, or opportunity. The challenge should exist beyond school and matter to people in a real context.
Choose something close enough for scholars to care about. The challenge should connect to scholars' lives, communities, futures, or sense of responsibility.
Make sure the challenge requires scholars to learn what the standards demand. Scholars should need to build disciplinary knowledge, use important concepts, and apply core skills in order to respond well.
Choose a challenge that can lead to meaningful action. The work should point toward designing, improving, informing, advocating, building, or testing something real.

There's no single entry point

In practice, challenge design moves in both directions. Begin with standards and find the right challenge, or begin with a real community issue and map what scholars will need to learn. Both work.

Start with the standards or content. Identify the core ideas, skills, and disciplinary practices scholars need to learn, then look for a real challenge that makes that learning necessary.
Start with the challenge. Identify a real issue scholars care about, then determine what standards-aligned learning scholars will need in order to understand it and respond credibly.
Move back and forth until the fit is strong. The best DEEDS challenges sit at the intersection of real need, scholar relevance, rigorous content, and meaningful action.

Bring the community in

Real partners — people living with the challenge, organizations already working on it, professionals with relevant expertise — make the work more accurate, more grounded, and more accountable. They also signal to scholars that what they're doing matters beyond the classroom.

Examples of the kinds of partners worth seeking out as you shape the work.
Partner type

Client experiencing the challenge

People living with the problem can help scholars understand what the challenge feels like, what is at stake, and what would actually be useful.

Ask yourselfWhat does this person need scholars to understand before they try to help?
Partner type

Organization already working on the issue

Community groups, nonprofits, agencies, or local institutions can help scholars understand what is already being tried, what constraints exist, and where there may be room to contribute.

Ask yourselfWhat can this organization help scholars see about the real conditions, gaps, or opportunities?
Partner type

Professional or expert perspective

Professionals bring disciplinary knowledge, criteria, tools, and feedback that help scholars do more credible work and understand how this kind of problem is approached beyond school.

Ask yourselfWhat expertise, standards, or feedback can this person offer that will strengthen the work?
Challenge Question

Frame the work as a question

The challenge question isn't just the starting point — it's the throughline. It names the real challenge, keeps the people served visible, and gives the work direction at every phase.

In some projects, the teacher begins with a draft question. In others, it's refined or co-crafted with scholars during Discover. Both approaches can work.

Design move: Start with a question sturdy enough to launch the work, then refine it as scholars deepen their understanding of the challenge, the context, and the people affected.
Challenge question frame
How might we address [a real challenge] so that [a specific community] can [experience meaningful improvement]?

The strongest questions are grounded in a real context, open enough to require investigation, and focused enough to guide a coherent project.

Make it real Ground the question in an actual place, partner, need, or condition.
Keep it open Scholars should need to learn, analyze, and investigate before they can respond well.
Point toward action The question should lead naturally to designing, improving, building, informing, or advocating.
Name who it serves Keep the people most affected visible throughout the learning journey.
