Challenge Design

Identify a relevant challenge

A strong DEEDS project starts with a challenge that is real, relevant, and actionable, but it also needs to hold the learning teachers are responsible for. Sometimes that means beginning with the standards or core content and looking for a challenge that gives that learning purpose.

Ground the work in an actual need, tension, problem, or opportunity. The challenge should exist beyond school and matter to people in a real context.
Choose something close enough for students to care about. The challenge should connect to students’ lives, communities, futures, or sense of responsibility.
Make sure the challenge requires students to learn what the standards demand. Students should need to build disciplinary knowledge, use important concepts, and apply core skills in order to respond well.
Choose a challenge that can lead to meaningful action. The work should point toward designing, improving, informing, advocating, building, or testing something real.

Teachers can start in more than one place

In practice, challenge design often moves in both directions. A teacher may begin with standards and content that must be taught, or begin with a real community issue and then map the learning students will need.

Start with the standards or content. Identify the core ideas, skills, and disciplinary practices students need to learn, then look for a real challenge that makes that learning necessary.
Start with the challenge. Identify a real issue students care about, then determine what standards-aligned learning students will need in order to understand it and respond credibly.
Move back and forth until the fit is strong. The best DEEDS challenges sit at the intersection of real need, student relevance, rigorous content, and meaningful action.
Partnership

Work with real partners

Partners might include people experiencing the challenge, organizations already working on the issue, or professionals with relevant expertise. Strong partners make the work more accurate, more grounded, and more accountable to the real world.

These are examples of the kinds of partners you might seek out as you shape the work.
Partner type

Client experiencing the challenge

People living with the problem can help students understand what the challenge feels like, what is at stake, and what would actually be useful.

What does this person need students to understand before they try to help?
Partner type

Organization already working on the issue

Community groups, nonprofits, agencies, or local institutions can help students understand what is already being tried, what constraints exist, and where there may be room to contribute.

What can this organization help students see about the real conditions, gaps, or opportunities?
Partner type

Professional or expert perspective

Professionals bring disciplinary knowledge, criteria, tools, and feedback that help students do more credible work and understand how this kind of problem is approached beyond school.

What expertise, standards, or feedback can this person offer that will strengthen the work?
Challenge Question

Write a strong challenge question

A DEEDS challenge question should name a real challenge, point toward action, and keep the people served visible throughout the learning.

In some projects, the teacher begins with a draft question. In others, the question is refined or co-crafted with students as they learn more during Discover. Both can be part of strong design.

Design move: Start with a question sturdy enough to launch the work, then refine it as students deepen their understanding of the challenge, the context, and the people affected.
Challenge question frame
How might we address [a real challenge] so that [a specific community] can [experience meaningful improvement]?
The strongest questions are grounded in a real context, open enough to require investigation, and focused enough to guide a coherent project.
Make it real Ground the question in an actual place, partner, need, or condition.
Keep it open Students should need to learn, analyze, and investigate before they can respond well.
Point toward action The question should lead naturally to designing, improving, building, informing, or advocating.
Name who it serves Keep the people most affected visible throughout the learning journey.
