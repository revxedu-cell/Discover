Make a Claim
Individually write an evidence-based claim about what your data shows about the impact or effectiveness of your solution.
One claim about the impact of our solution: ___
Evidence that supports this claim: ___
One limit, tradeoff, or unanswered question our data raises: ___

Claim Sentence Starters

This likely happened because…
This worked best when…, which suggests…
This did not work in cases where…, possibly due to…

The Point

A claim without evidence is just an opinion. A Data Dive helps you move from "it worked" or "it didn't" to a specific, evidence-backed explanation that others can learn from.

Remember

Imperfect results are expected and valuable. The goal is understanding — not proving that your solution was perfect. Honest analysis of what didn't work is just as important as celebrating what did.

Teacher Background · Do
Data Dive — Facilitator Guide
Analysis, evidence-based reasoning, scientific explanation · NGSS SEP 4, SEP 6 · CCSS ELA R.7 · C3 D4

Standards Alignment

NGSS SEP 4 Analyzing and interpreting data — students examine real-world data from their implementation to identify patterns, trends, and anomalies.
NGSS SEP 6 Constructing explanations — the Make a Claim step produces evidence-backed explanations of impact and causal reasoning.
CCSS R.7 Integrating and evaluating content in diverse formats — students interpret quantitative and qualitative data from multiple sources.
C3 D4 Communicating and critiquing conclusions — students write claims and evaluate their data for limitations and competing interpretations.

Why This Routine — SOLD Alignment

S
Social: The Observe and Notice Patterns steps are deliberately collaborative — students build a shared understanding of the data before writing individual claims. This prevents isolated or skewed interpretation.
O
Ownership: Students write their own claims individually, even after group discussion. This preserves intellectual agency and prepares each scholar to defend their reasoning in Share.
L
Lived Experience: The data comes from the real community challenge students have been investigating. Analysis is grounded in evidence about people and places students know and care about.
D
Disciplinary Thinking: Scientists, evaluators, and policy researchers conduct exactly this kind of structured data analysis. The routine teaches authentic investigative practice.

Marzano Connection

  • Identifying Similarities and Differences: Notice Patterns requires comparative thinking — students examine change over time or across conditions.
  • Generating and Testing Hypotheses: The Claim step formalizes hypothesis-testing by requiring students to pair observations with causal explanations.
  • Note-taking and Summarizing: Students write a structured claim with evidence, practicing the synthesis moves central to academic writing and standardized assessments.

Facilitation Notes

  • Protect the Observe step — don't let students jump to explanation before they've described what the data literally shows. Prompt: "What do you see — not what you think it means yet?"
  • Prepare data visuals in advance when possible (a projected graph, feedback summary, or observation log) so groups don't spend the 8-min observe step just reading raw notes.
  • Normalize imperfect results explicitly. Say: "Finding out something didn't fully work is the most useful result you can get. That's science." This reduces defensiveness.
  • For younger scholars, scaffold the Claim step with a printed sentence frame sheet at each table. The three-part structure (Claim / Evidence / Limit) maps directly to disciplinary writing across subjects.
  • Share out one or two claims whole-class after individual writing. Ask the group to evaluate: Does the evidence actually support the claim? What questions does it raise?

When to Use It

At the close of every Do session during the implementation period — especially when you're gathering data over multiple days. It keeps observation habits sharp and surfaces insights before they're forgotten.

Format

Quick Turn & Talk with a partner or small group. Not a full writing task — the goal is to name and hold onto what matters before you leave. Brief notes in your data log are enough.

Why It Matters

Surprises are data. When something unexpected happens during implementation, that's often the most important thing you learned. This routine teaches you to notice and name it — not dismiss it.

Teacher Background · Do
Evidence Snapshot — Facilitator Guide
Metacognition, observation habits, formative reflection · NGSS SEP 3 · CCSS SL.1 · C3 D3

Standards Alignment

NGSS SEP 3 Planning and carrying out investigations — daily observation during implementation is an authentic scientific practice; Evidence Snapshot builds that habit.
CCSS SL.1 Collaborative discussion — the Turn & Talk format requires students to articulate evidence and share observations with precision in dialogue.
C3 D3.3 Gathering and evaluating sources — students prioritize and evaluate the significance of individual pieces of evidence relative to their question.
Math SMP.6 Attend to precision — naming evidence precisely (what, where, when observed) builds mathematical and scientific precision habits.

Why This Routine — SOLD Alignment

S
Social: The Turn & Talk format creates a low-stakes peer conversation daily. Partners catch observations the individual missed and surface disagreements about what the data shows.
O
Ownership: Students select their own "most important evidence" — this requires judgment, not recall. It treats scholars as active evaluators of their own data, not passive recorders.
L
Lived Experience: Surprises during real-world implementation are often tied to community complexity — things students didn't anticipate because of assumptions. Naming surprises builds honest self-awareness.
D
Disciplinary Thinking: Field researchers, social workers, and program evaluators debrief after every observation session. This routine teaches that discipline.

Marzano Connection

  • Reinforcing Effort and Providing Recognition: Naming "what surprised me" validates the difficulty of real-world implementation — students learn that unexpected results signal growth, not failure.
  • Cues, Questions, and Advance Organizers: The three prompts serve as structured advance organizers for the full Data Dive — students who complete daily Evidence Snapshots arrive at the Data Dive session with richer observations.
  • Setting Objectives: The "Consider Next Steps" prompt is a micro-planning move — it sets observation intentions for the next session and reinforces deliberate, focused data collection.

Facilitation Notes

  • Keep it genuinely quick — 3 minutes is enough if students know the prompts. Post them visibly in the room during the Do phase so no time is lost re-reading them each day.
  • Call on 2–3 pairs to share their "surprise" each day. This builds a class-wide observation log that can be referenced during the full Data Dive.
  • If students consistently have "no surprises," probe further: "What went exactly as expected? How do you know your prediction was right?"
  • For extended implementation periods (multi-week projects), brief weekly Data Dives can be anchored by accumulating Evidence Snapshot notes from the daily routine.
  • The Next Steps prompt directly feeds session planning. Consider collecting these briefly and using them to shape the next session's structure or focus.
